# The S&P 500 is a concentrated tech bet with 490 companies stapled to it

**Date:** 2026-04-29
**Source:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=NyVBWIW6vbk

---

You've been told to buy index funds and chill.

Dollar cost average into the S&P 500. Don't try to beat the market. Let compound interest do its thing over 30 years.

It's not bad advice exactly. But Codie Sanchez recently made a point that stopped me cold: 10 companies now control 28% of the entire S&P 500.

Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Meta, Alphabet, Tesla, and a few others depending on the week. That's not diversification. That's a concentrated tech portfolio with 490 other companies stapled to it.

When you "buy the market," you're betting those specific companies will dominate for decades. Maybe they will. But markets are littered with companies that looked invincible at their peak.

Kodak controlled 90% of the film market in the 1990s. Filed for bankruptcy in 2012. General Electric was the most valuable company on Earth. Today it's a shadow of itself.

None of the top 10 companies from 20 years ago are in the top 10 now.

**The deeper problem**

Here's what actually made me uncomfortable: in 1996, there were 8,000 publicly listed companies in America. Today, it's roughly half that.

The average company used to go public after 8 years. Now it's 12. And that gap matters more than it sounds.

A law finance review put it bluntly: by the time companies finally list, much of the growth phase has already passed. The 10x to 100x run that used to happen after IPO now happens before it, in private rounds you and I aren't invited to.

Amazon went public at a $400 million valuation. Investors rode it to hundreds of billions. Today's equivalent companies are listing at $10 billion, $50 billion, $100 billion. The venture capitalists and private equity funds have already eaten the upside.

By the time a company shows up in your brokerage app with a shiny ticker, you're not early. You're the exit liquidity.

**Why founders stopped wanting to go public**

Being a public company CEO has become genuinely miserable.

The compliance costs alone run $10-40 million annually for a billion-dollar company. Audits, lawyers, quarterly earnings rituals. That's before the humiliation of standing in front of analysts every 90 days, knowing one bad quarter can wipe billions off your stock price.

Codie shared a brutal example. Activist investor Dan Loeb once wrote a public letter calling a CEO "one of the most dangerous and incompetent executives in America." These letters get filed with the SEC, picked up by press, read by employees and family. The point isn't just to change strategy. It's to make staying in the job socially unbearable.

So when venture capital and private equity exploded, offering founders all the capital they needed without the circus, many of them simply stopped going public.

SpaceX. Stripe. Cargill. Some of the most important companies in America have never listed. They're not in your index fund. They're in rich people's portfolios instead.

**The math that changed my perspective**

Here's the question Codie posed that I keep thinking about:

To generate $200,000 per year in retirement income from index funds at a 4% withdrawal rate, you need $5 million invested.

Or you could own three boring businesses doing $2 million in revenue each with 15% margins. That's $300,000 per year.

Which game requires less luck?

The quietly wealthy people, the ones with paid-off houses who don't check prices at dinner, mostly didn't get there through index funds. They got there owning private businesses.

Index funds are where you park money. Private markets are where you make it.

**Why this matters for 2026**

Private equity is stuck right now. Through the 2010s and early 2020s, PE firms went on a buying spree when interest rates were near zero. They planned to hold companies for 3-5 years, improve them, sell to the next buyer.

Then rates rose. Credit tightened. The buyers who were supposed to take those businesses off their hands largely disappeared.

There are currently over 16,000 companies held by PE firms for more than four years. Average hold time has stretched to 6.5 years. These firms have limited partners demanding returns. They need to sell.

When motivated sellers meet informed buyers, deals favour the buyer. That's just how markets work when leverage shifts.

**Where this leaves us**

I'm not saying sell your index funds. For most people, they're still a reasonable default for money you won't touch for decades.

But the "just buy the S&P and relax" advice assumes the game hasn't changed. It has. The index is more concentrated than ever. The best companies stay private longer. And the wealth-building opportunities have quietly moved somewhere most retail investors never look.

The Anywhere Income approach has always been about building multiple income streams, not betting everything on one path. Your index fund can be one bucket. But it probably shouldn't be the only one.

If nothing else, it's worth understanding what you're actually buying when you "buy the market."
